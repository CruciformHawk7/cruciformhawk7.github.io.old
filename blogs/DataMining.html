Data Mining in 2050

Startling to a heap of clothes on a chair after a nap, thinking that it was someone watching you sleep. We all have had this happen to us. Human minds love to recognise patterns. There are various theories revolving around this, but the reason is clear: It is better to see a danger and be prepared than waste time admiring the beauty of something

If a lion were to stalk you, hiding in a bush, you'd notice the lion's face and immediately run, rather than wait and look at what species of lion it is, and calculate what your chances of being attacked are. Recognising patterns helps humans understand what to expect next.

Humans have lived in the wild as primates, and this behaviour is what we are shaped with - we try to perceive patterns on almost anything. The things you see, the music you listen to, the food you eat, etc. Learning patterns helps humans understand the situation, and predict what is next.

Humans learn from patterns too. We look at the alphabet as a young child, and now we know how the letter A looks, no matter how obscure it looks. We learn this from experience. We interact with each other in a similar fashion. When you read this article, notice how these "words" are just a bunch of letters together. Letters separated doesn't make any sense, however when put together, they give an implicit meaning; its a pattern of letters together that makes some sense to you.

Humans rely on computer machines for their everyday task. Computers, unlike Humans, don't quite work on patterns, but rather combination of switches, or to simplify, purely on mathematics. To make these mathematical wonders make sense of data the way you do, you need to make it think like a human. Fortunately, great scientists have discovered various ways in which computers make sense of our data. This science of teaching computers to learn and recognise patterns and make sense, decipher, and predict is dealt in Data Mining.

Data mining is used my almost all the gadgets around you. Advertisements that you see on almost all the pages tailored to your recommendations always go through the data mining process to get ads of products you might like. These days, data mining isn't the game of the big companies.

Chances are that the local supermarket knows by a chance how likely you are going to buy a product. Some might call this privacy intrusion, but if you teach a computer to think like you, it surely can predict what you think.
